Background & Motivation

In recent years, there was “Pokemon Go” craze -- adults and children starred at their phones and meandered around town searching for the cartoon creatures. The augmented reality technology behind the game took the world by store. Although the public became acquainted with AR just a few years ago, the technology was actually developed in the early 1990s. If you look closely, we all have seen the AR before the Pokemon Go. For instance, the yellow line that highlights the distance between the ball and the first-down mark in a football game, the research labs in sci-fi movies that have virtual computers screen pop-up in the middle of the room. These are the example of the uses of AR in the early days. But what really is AR? The formal definition of Augmented reality or AR is “a direct or indirect live view of a physical, real-world environment whose elements are "augmented" by computer-generated perceptual information, ideally across multiple sensory modalities.”

AR can easily be the next big thing. According to International Data Corporation(IDC) projections, global spending on products and services related to augmented reality and virtual reality will grow more than 100 percent annually; jumping from $11.4 billion in 2017 to $214 billion in 2021 (Forbes, 2017). The number is ridiculously high because the growing applications/uses of AR. For instance, IKEA Place lets you virtually place IKEA products in your space, so you can get an idea whether the new furniture you plan to buy will suite your room or not. The growth of the AR is exciting and inspired us to jump head first into this new and emerging technology. Moreover, we can apply concepts from our curriculum to create a useful application that can improve the Penn State experience.
